Brief History of Toronto

Toronto was founded in 1793 by Governor John Graves Simcoe and is now the largest city in Canada, the third largest in North America. Governor John Graves Simcoe was told to create a new city farther away from the United States as a new capital. Simcoe wanted to create the new capital where kitchener is today, but was denied due to lack of water access. While surveying the land, he got lost and a group of natives who knew his father helped him find his way to lake Ontario. He surveyed this land, knew it was far away from any land border with the United States and saw that it was sheltered by the Toronto islands, unable to be seen from lands and potential invading ships. Knowing the potential need for evacuation, he devised a plan for a straight road that would run north up until Lake Simcoe, named after his father, that would allow them to escape safely. To build this road and start the city, Governer Simcoe started giving land lots to wealthy loyalists with the requirement that at the center where Yonge street is, the owners had to maintain the road and they could not sell off land until a year after they got the land.

North Toronto

The Town of North Toronto was incorporated in 1890 as an amalgamation of Davisville Village, Eglinton Village, and Bedford Park Village. At the time, the land was primarily for agricultural farming, but by the early 1900's, North Toronto had emerged as one of Toronto's most popular commuter suburbs. When frustration due to poor level of municipal services offered by the Town of North Toronto increased, North Toronto residents voted in favour of Annexation to the City of Toronto on December 15, 1912. North Toronto grew quickly and by the 1940's, was completely developed and no longer an agricultural area.

Yonge And Eglinton Intersection

Now home to the Yonge And Eglinton Malls, 50 story high condos and the Yonge and Eglinton Subway station, soon to be connected to the Eglinton subway, it was not always that way. Before the streetcar was added it was nothing but an intersection dividing North Toronto and York, surrounded by endless fields. When the streetcar was extended to Yonge And Eglinton after the annexation of North Toronto, Yonge and Eglinton started to grow. In 1954, Yonge and Eglinton Subway station opened, bringing even more people to Yonge and Eglinton. In 1973, the Yonge Eglinton Centre, the mall, opened up, a major attraction full of over 65 stores and a Cineplex.

Duplex and Eglinton Intersection

As traffic increased due to the new streetcar increasing the population and the easy access to carriages, Yonge Avenue was not enough to allow for the flow of a growing city and Yonge and Eglinton was constantly busy. Many people were getting stuck in traffic, something that still occurs to this day, and in an attempt to ease the traffic, Duplex Avenue was added in 1915. Connecting from Davisville all the way to Lawrence, Duplex allowed for another way to travel through the city and access Downtown.

Original Post Office

Post offices are essential for the marking of a village as it gave them access to communication with other areas nearby and around Canada. The original Post Office for the Eglinton Village was at the North east corner of Keewatin and Yonge, then at the amalgamation into North Toronto, the post office moved to the South east corner of Keewatin and Yonge. In 1920, the post office moved to Montgomery Square, home of the famous Montgomery Tavern, where it stayed open until 2012. It is now opening a condo with the base keeping the post office.
